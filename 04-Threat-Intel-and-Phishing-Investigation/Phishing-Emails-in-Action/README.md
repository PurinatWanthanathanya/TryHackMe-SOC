# Lab: Phishing Emails in Action

**Role:** Junior Security Analyst

## 🎯 Objective
To analyze and investigate real-world phishing campaigns, identify social engineering tactics, and detect malicious indicators within email headers, URLs, and file attachments.

## 🚀 Execution Steps
1. **Email Header & Source Analysis:** 
   * Investigated the origin of suspicious emails by analyzing sender addresses and headers.
   * Identified email spoofing techniques used by attackers to impersonate trusted entities and bypass initial security filters.
2. **Content & URL Investigation:** 
   * Examined email bodies for psychological triggers (e.g., urgency, fear) commonly used in fake payment notifications (e.g., PayPal, Netflix, Apple) and fake document services (e.g., Citrix).
   * Inspected embedded links for URL manipulation and uncovered the use of tracking pixels utilized by adversaries to monitor user engagement.
3. **Malicious Attachment Triage:** 
   * Analyzed suspicious file attachments, including PDFs, Microsoft Word templates, and Excel files.
   * Identified the presence of malicious macros and hidden malware payloads designed to execute upon opening the documents.

## 📊 Results & Evidence
* Successfully dissected multiple phishing emails, identifying the true source of spoofed addresses and uncovering credential harvesting portals disguised as legitimate corporate services.

## 🧠 Key Takeaways
* **The Role of Human Psychology:** Recognized that sophisticated phishing attacks do not just exploit technical vulnerabilities; they heavily exploit human emotions like urgency and fear to manipulate users into taking immediate action.
* **Malicious Payloads in Everyday Files:** Learned that ordinary-looking files (Word, Excel, PDF) are frequently weaponized with macros or malicious templates, proving that technical defenses alone are not foolproof.
* **Verification is Key:** Developed a critical mindset for defensive security, understanding that verifying the true source of an email and carefully inspecting URLs before clicking are essential practices to thwart credential harvesting and malware infections.
